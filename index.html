2008 Housing Crisis

Introduction

The 2008 housing crisis was a global financial crisis triggered by the collapse of the housing bubble in the United States. It led to a severe economic downturn and widespread mortgage defaults, affecting millions of people. Below are key elements that contributed to the crisis:

  • Interest and Inflation: Low interest rates encouraged borrowing, while rising inflation raised household costs.
  • Consumer Price Index (CPI): Rising consumer costs strained household budgets, including housing expenses.
  • HPI by State: Home Price Index varied by state, with some areas experiencing unsustainable increases.
  • Vacant Properties: Falling home prices led to an increase in vacant properties as foreclosures rose.
  • Foreclosure: Subprime mortgages and unaffordable payments led to a surge in foreclosures, further depressing prices.
  • Rent Occupied: Foreclosures increased demand for rental properties, driving up rental prices.
  • Unemployment: Job losses across sectors exacerbated economic uncertainty and reduced consumer spending.

Impact

The crisis had devastating effects on both homeowners and the global economy. Millions lost their homes, unemployment soared, and financial markets collapsed, leading to the worst recession since the Great Depression.
